Grocery Basket Analytics Case Study: Exploratory Data Analysis

 

June 1, 2017

 
 

In this case study, we will explore real life sales dataset of a grocery retailer and try to quickly generate insights and answer several questions without asking. The case study is in two parts: first, we will do rapid visual analysis and then we will demonstrate how machine learning models can help us generate not so obvious insights.

 

Without much further ado, let's begin. 

 

Review the Input Dataset:

First step is to load and quickly understand the input data set files. The input dataset is provided as text files of anonymized transaction records of around 200k customers stored in comma separated vector format. The dataset contains of following files:

1. Transaction Orders (3.4 million records) - Order ID, Customer ID, Date, Hour timestamp

2. Order Products (~35 million records): Order ID, Product ID, Add to Cart Order

3. Products (50,000 records): Product ID, Product Name, Aisle ID, Department ID

4. Aisles (134 records): Aisle ID, Aisle Name

5. Departments (14 records): Department ID, Department Name

 

There are no names of the customers or any personal details which reveal the identity. For our quick analysis below, we took a stratified sample dataset of about 1.5 million records. 

 

So let's begin answering:

 

How is the Store Organized?
Let's first quickly make a visual tour of the store. We map the store as per departments and aisles within the departments. The size of each aisle is the number of SKUs or unique products stored. We find that Snacks, Personal Care and Pantry departments together account for almost one-third of total products. This is followed by Beverages, Frozen and Dairy/Eggs departments. 

 

 

To get a different perspective, we now re-scale the store map for the number of unique products sold (instead of stored). We find that the Produce department witnesses highest turnover followed by Dairy/Eggs department. Together these two departments account for over one-third of total products turned over by the store. 

 

 

 

What is the Busy-ness Pattern of the Store? 

Let's investigate which are the peak hours and which are the dull hours for the store. By plotting a heatmap of the store in terms of transaction activity, we find that the store witnesses highest activity during the weekends, continued activity on 1st day of the week and then slows down towards mid-week before picking up again gradually. Late night and early mornings remain low-activity and peak hours are from 7am to 6pm with weekend business remaining active until later hours. 

 

 

What Draws Customers to the Store?

Let's add some color to our understanding on what brings customers back to the store. We see in the chart below that the customers visit the store most often for convenience of purchasing fresh produce - vegetables, fruits , dairy/eggs, beverages, bakery and other perishable food items.   

 

 

We can take a more granular look by observing aisles with highest repurchase frequency and the ones with lowest. We find that sparkling water, eggs, cream, yogurt and bread have the highest repurchase frequency whereas kitchen supplies, cold/flu alergy, skin care, food storage and baby bath body care have the lowest repurchase frequency.  

 

Here it could also be useful to change the perspective a bit and look at the repurchasing by mapping both departments and aisles . We observe details such as within 'Babies' department, the repurchase frequency for 'baby bath body care' aisle is much less than that of 'baby accessories' aisle and in 'Household' department, the repurchase frequency from 'kitchen supplies' aisle is much less than 'paper goods' aisle. 

  

 

 

Let's Peel Another Layer:

After we have visually scanned the landscape, we are now ready to formulate various lines of inquiry and investigate further.

 

How Often Customers Revisit?

For example, we may want to understand better the revisit frequency of customers to the store. We find that there is a pronounced weekly trend of customers returning to the store. This is likely the weekend shopper group. A significant number of customers return to the store same day every week however there is a decent category of customers who come same day every second week and some every third week. The number beyond 30 is aggregate of all who are infrequent visitors. 

 

As another line of inquiry, we may want to see if there is any particular relation between the product first put in the cart and the repurchase frequency. We find that there is indeed a link. The product first put in the cart does have highest repurchase frequency.   

We also observe that there are certain products which are put much later in the cart but have higher repurchase frequency. We may want to investigate that further and it opens few additional lines of inquiry. But for now, we will not go any further in this direction.  

 

Product Affinity Analysis:

Let's investigate what are the products that have affinity with each other in the consumer basket. In order to do so, we feed the dataset to models and identify frequent itemsets and create association rules.

 

We observe the distribution of number of products in customer basket and also the products with highest frequency of occurence in the customer basket. These help us to set the support levels to calibrate our machine learning models.   

 

 

 

Create Association Rules:Let's determine frequent itemsets and mine some association rules between them. We set the support threshold to low value to cast a wide net (remember that the number of transaction baskets is quite large, so even these low support levels indicate tens of thousand baskets.)  

We find that there are lot of bananas in the sets. This is not surprising given that it is one of the most frequently purchased item, but it does not help us very much with analysis. 

 

So we mine for our association rules based on low support, high confidence and look for lift. Here we find some very interesting patterns with high lift indicating strong association rules. But we notice that these products are also very close in nature. We should go a step further and do some pre-processing by creating groups of similar products to have a more refined analysis. However for this illustrative case study, we will not pursue it any further.    

 

 

Affinity Analysis by Aisle:

We can also do affinity analysis by aisles instead of products to have a coarser view. Below is a snapshot of the basket analysis if a product from Chips/Pretzels aisle is in the basket. Besides the frequently moving items, we find that there is an affinity with fresh dips tapenades. Not a revolutionary finding though!

  

But there can be some not to obvious findings. While observing affinity of products with Ice Creams, we find that energy granola bars have a high probability of being purchased by the same customer. 

 

Such affiinity analysis have several uses. It can help identify targetted actions at operational levels and it can also help in decision making for which products to store and where. In business combinations and M&A situations, such analysis can help identify product gaps, latent sales potential, synergies and other value drivers.

 

Conclusion: 

We conducted a rapid visual analysis of the dataset and made interesting and useful observations. Then we applied machine learning methods to mine for association rules and found some not so obvious patterns. Each investigation revealed several details that needed further investigation.

 

With the help of visual analytics and machine learning, we were able to quickly analyze, confirm several of our findings and generate insights. We feel more confident to present our analysis and recommendations.
