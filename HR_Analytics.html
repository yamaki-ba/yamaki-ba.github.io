HR Analytics Case Study: Why are people leaving and predict who will leave

 

May 23, 2017

 
 

In this case study, we will explore a real life HR dataset of a company and apply analytics to investigate why people are leaving there. The case study is in two parts: first, we will use human intelligence do visual analysis and then we will build machine learning model to compare our findings and select a model to make predictions.

 

So without much further ado, let's begin. 

 

Load the Dataset:

First step is to load and quickly understand the input data set file. The dataset consists of anonymised details of employees.

 

We observe key stats and distribution of data. There are 15,000 employee records as a partial dataset for the company.

 

 

The features are whether still employed, department, salary band, average monthly hours worked, last evaluation score, no of projects/ workload score, Whether promoted in last 5 years, satisfaction score from appraisals and no of years spent in the company. 

 

Preparing the Data:

Fortunately, the dataset appears mostly clean. For analysis, we do some data preparation. The records with missing information are removed and the salary bands are converted to three bands: low, medium and high. 

 

Visualize the Dataset:

Let's Begin by first evaluating the spread of information contained in the feature set for analysis. We prepare a quick correlation matrix to see if there is some duplication of information. We find that there is not much correlation between attributes. So we do not face multicollinearity challenge and will not do any dimentionality reduction -- which we would have done if the dimentions showed strong correlations.    

On observing the matrix, we find that strongest (negative) correlation of employees leaving appears to be with satisfaction level, which is not surprising. We also see that Salary levels seem to be the second most negatively correlated factor.

 

So we have begun discovering. Let's continue the journey. We make a dashboard to scan our data visually.  

  We make some quick useful observations and note them for future use:

- employees appear largely satisfied,

- average monthly working hours have median around 200. It appears though that there are several employees that work much longer hours and others less hours. 

- Last evaluation is centered around 0.7 score.

- Workload wise mean is around 3.75-3.80 projects per person, but some appear overloaded and few underworked. 

- Median employee has spent around three and half years with the company and it appears that retention rates are low beyond 4 years. 

 

Continue visual analysis:

Let's continue to find any more patterns if we can. So we make more charts to observe any clues. 

First we observe if there are any patterns of leaving employees with departments.

 

We observe that that while sales has highest number of staff leaving, as a % of total HR and Accounts have highest turnover rates. Rest departments appears around 22%-25% turnover rates. R&D and management has best retention rates.

 

Ok. so we refer from our notes that satisfaction and salary had strongest relation to employees leaving. So let's see if there is a pattern of salary and satisfaction with departments:

 

We observe from the bottom chart that average satisfaction score across all departments are not materially different but accounts and HR have slightly lower scores. Both these departments have higher rate of leaving. 

 

We look at the top charts and observe that retention rates are much better in higher salary band. Medium and lower salary bands have higher attrition rates. 

 

So it appears that satisfaction and salary may have something more to tell. So let's plot that. 


 

 

We find that there is indeed a link between satisfaction and salary. Higher paid employees seem to be more satisfied than the lower salary band employees.  

This is not a surprising find though. 

 

We had also observed that workload had something to do with employees leaving. So let's plot that and look for clues:

 

There is something very interesting that we observe here. On the top box chart, we find that satisfaction score of employees working more than others is markedly dropped! The employees having less work are also less satisfied. There is a middle band which seems to be satisfied. 

 

The bottom chart - evaluation vs workload - shows gives another very interesting insight. All departments seem to be rewarding their overworked staff except accounting department where the evaluation score of the overworked employees seem to fall!       

 

It appears that insights have begun to emerge. 

 

We had also noted earlier that the attrition rate of employees have something to do with their length of employment in the company. So let's visualize that:

 

Some points pops out there. We note unusual leaving patterns with length of service in hige salary band of R&D and support department. 

 

This brings us to end of our initial analysis of the data. We noted some important points which would be helpful in review of HR policies of various departments.  

  

Handover to Machine Learning:

So let's build a machine learning model to evaluate the dataset and test if we can predict the leaving employees from the avaialble attribute information. 

 

We will do split validation with 30% records of the dataset to train our selected model and apply the model to rest of the dataset.  

 

So here is a simple map of our test model:

 

We try Decision Tree, Random Forest and Naive Bayes here and measure our accuracy and precision. We find that all of our selected models perform well and give high scores.

 

To avoid risk of overfitting to the current dataset and make sure that our model performs well with unseen data, we pick Decision Tree of maximum depth of 5 levels.  

 

We find the performance to be quite encouraging with:

 

Accuracy: 91%

Precision: 95.6%

Precision (0): 91% 

Precision (1): 96%.

So we do not have a class bias and good predictability. 

 

Conclusion:

Decision attributes picked by the model are satisfaction level, number of projects/workload, average monthly hours and last evaluation. 

 

Interestingly, we find that our initial observation of importance of salary and department does not appear as a factor in employees leaving. It comes out that the major reason for employees leaving is the balance between workload and evaluation.  

 

With the help of visual analytics and machine learning, we were able to quickly analyze, confirm several of our findings and generate insights. We feel more confident to present our analysis and recommendations.

 

Recommendations:

The company should take employee satisfaction scores more seriously. There is a need to evaluate how project work is being allocated, especially in Accounting and HR department. The employees are not being rewarded for their extra work and the company should find a balance between workload and salary of employees. The prediction model can accurately give the list of employees that are likely to leave to evaluate on urgent basis.
